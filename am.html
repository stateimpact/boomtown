Police Chief Randy Epler grew up in Towanda. He says the crimes committed in town haven't changed much over the years but attitudes about DUI enforcement and gun rights are different among new residents.

More gas wells have been drilled in Bradford County than in any other Pennsylvania county.

Drilling initially boosted the economy but falling natural gas prices means less drilling, less work, and less money.

Some residents say since drilling began the sleepy small town feel of Towanda, home to roughly 3,000 people, has disappeared.

Rent has increased exponentially over the last five years, pricing many Towandans with minimum wage jobs out of town.

Barbara Keeney (left) and Karen Parkhurst run The Weigh Station restaurant. Their restaurant caters to locals, tourists on the way to Niagra Falls and drillers.

Every morning a group of men gather for coffee and conversation at the Weigh Station. Many of the men are lifelong Towandans and conversation often turns to changes in their town.

"More people tend to lock their vehicles," says Mike Miller (far right) speaking of changes in Towanda since drilling arrived, "there just seems to be the feeling that you need a little more security.

Don Brenner performs during open mic night at the Weigh Station.

The walls of the Weigh Station are covered in signatures of patrons from all over the world.

“Landmen” flocked to the county courthouse in 2007 and 2008 to dig up information about Bradford County property.

Trucks carry drilling equipment cross the Susquehanna River. During the height of drilling activity traffic would back up across the bridge for three miles coming into Towanda.

The Weigh Station chefs took a Mexican cooking class and changed their menu to offer a taste of home to drilling workers from the Southwest.

C&G Wilcox Engraving and Images is a 101-year-old family run business. Customers spend thousands of dollars on the drilling inspired rings and necklaces.

Key chains of rigs, drill bits, and other drilling related items are some of the cheaper items for sale.

The New Shoe Store Plus on Main Street changed the types of boots and clothing they carry to reflect the desires of gas drillers.

According to shoe store employee, Jan Millard, they never used to sell “pull on boots” until workers from the Southwest started requesting them.

Garcia’s is the only Mexican restaurant in Towanda. The lunch crowd is mostly drilling workers but the evening crowd is a broader mix of workers from the nearby DuPont and GTP plants.

Chesapeake is one of the main drilling companies working in Bradford County.

Global Tungsten & Powders produces a range of powders and alloys and is one of the major employers in Towanda.

The DuPont manufacturing plant is another main employer in Towanda.

The number of commitments to the Bradford Co. Correctional Facility jumped from 895 in 2008 to 1,065 in 2011. It is projected that there will be 1,319 commitments in 2012.

Towanda has experienced booms in industries before first with logging, then coal mining and then agriculture. However, each boom has come with a bust.

A mounted buck named Oscar hangs on the wall at the Bradford County Museum.

You could find a 1 bedroom apartment in Towanda for $275-300 in 2008. In 2011 rent for a 1 bedroom apartment goes for 1,000+

Joe Benjamin grew up in Towanda. He makes upwards of $20 an hour but can’t afford rent in town so he lives with his girlfriend in her mother’s basement.

Judy Smith has been the volunteer program director of Grace Connections for the last 7 years. Grace Connections helps needy Towandans by putting them up in motels and providing food vouchers.

Volunteers pack bags of food at the Towanda Area Christian Outreach (TACO) office.

TACO provides emergency food relief and a monthly food pantry for needy Towandans.

When you ask a Towandan what is the biggest change since drilling arrived nearly every person answers, "traffic."

Heavy duty truck traffic clogs the two lane Main Street but drilling companies have made major improvements to area roads by widening and re-paving previously worn routes.

A man sits outside of Fairchild’s Hardware store, one of the oldest family owned businesses in Towanda.

Business has slowed at the Weigh Station but the owners are thankful for the few extra years of good profits.

Towanda high school has hired a fulltime ESL teacher, because of the drilling boom. About 30 new students have transferred to the school, but a third of them have already moved elsewhere.

Fog rolls off the Susquehanna River early in the morning as traffic crosses the bridge entering Towanda.
